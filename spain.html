Part of our time is spent in Galicia, where we are working on the renovation of a derelict stone farmhouse. A personal project, and a direct introduction to the Galician building culture.

Background

Why Galicia

Galicia had been pulling at us for years before we did anything about it. The landscape, the architecture, the particular quality of the light, and the fact that you can still find buildings here that have been standing for three hundred years and cost less than a London bathroom renovation. In 2025 we bought one: a derelict two-storey granite farmhouse on the edge of Negreira, A Coruña. It was partially collapsed internally, the floor structure had failed, and the site was completely overgrown. We bought it anyway.

The renovation is ongoing. We are working through it in phases: clearing the site, stabilising the structure, navigating planning permissions, sourcing local contractors, learning the aparejador process. It is slower than expected and more interesting than anticipated. Working through the Galician building system at first hand gives you a practical knowledge of it that is genuinely difficult to get any other way.

Because of that, I now work with a small number of international buyers who are at the beginning of the same process. Not as a service business, but because the questions they are asking are the ones we asked, and I can give them honest answers.

The project

Casa Negreira

A two-storey granite rubble masonry building on the edge of Negreira, bought in 2025 in a state of partial collapse. The floor structure had failed on the upper level. The site was overgrown to the point of being impassable. The roof was holding, just.

We are restoring it in phases, working with local contractors and sourcing traditional materials where possible. The approach is restrained: stone walls cleaned and repointed in lime, handmade terracotta on the ground floor, the upper level opened to the roof ridge to create a double-height volume. We are not in a hurry. The building has been here long enough to deserve some patience.

Notes on the process: what we have learned, what we got wrong, what the planning system actually involves, are published in the journal.

For international buyers

Buying in Galicia or Asturias?

These are not straightforward places to buy property if you have not done it before. The legal process is different, the building culture is different, and many of the properties that attract international buyers have been neglected for decades. The risks are real and they are not always obvious from a viewing.

Having been through the process ourselves, I work with a small number of buyers and owners at the stages where an architect's input is most useful: before you commit to a purchase, when you are trying to understand what a building actually needs, and when you are ready to start work but do not know how the system operates.
