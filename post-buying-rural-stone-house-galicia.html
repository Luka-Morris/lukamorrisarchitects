What to Look For When Buying a Rural Stone House in Galicia

Galicia's rural stone houses have a way of getting under your skin. The granite walls, the slate roofs, the feeling that the building has been there long enough to have earned its place in the landscape. All of that is true. What is also true is that some of them are held together more by optimism than mortar, and knowing the difference before you sign anything is the whole game.

First, some context

Most of the rural property stock in Galicia predates formal planning regulation by a considerable margin. These buildings were put up by local builders using whatever granite came out of the ground nearby, bonded with lime mortar mixed on site, roofed with slate from the nearest quarry. They were not engineered in any formal sense. They were built by people who understood the materials intuitively, and who had no particular reason to write anything down.

The result is a building stock that is often extraordinary in its robustness and completely opaque in its condition. You cannot look at a Galician stone house from the road and know whether the floors are solid or whether they will take your weight. You cannot tell from the outside whether the roof structure is intact or whether it has been quietly composting for fifteen years. The walls, which can be a metre thick, give nothing away.

Add to this a post-pandemic surge in interest from northern European buyers, prices that have moved substantially in accessible rural areas, and a legal and planning system that operates entirely in Spanish, and you have a purchase that rewards careful preparation and punishes the assumption that it works like buying a house at home.

It does not. But it is worth it, if you go in with your eyes open.

What the structure is actually telling you

Granite rubble masonry is a forgiving material. It has been standing in wet climates for centuries, and it will continue to stand for centuries more if treated with any basic respect. The problems are almost never the stone itself. They are the mortar, the roof, the timber, and the water that gets in when any of those three are neglected.

The walls

Start at the corners. The interlocking quoin stones at the external corners of the building are what tie the two wall faces together into a single structure. Vertical cracking at the corners tells you the walls have begun to separate and are no longer working together. That is a structural conversation, not a cosmetic one.

Then look at the faces of the walls for bulging or bowing, particularly on gable ends, which are the tallest and least supported sections of the building. A plumb line or simply a trained eye will catch this. Any visible curvature deserves investigation before you go further.

Pay attention to the mortar joints. Bring a thin tool to every viewing and press it gently into the joints. If it goes in easily, the mortar has failed and the wall is held together by friction, gravity, and the goodwill of the stones. This is not necessarily a dealbreaker. Lime repointing is standard practice in Galicia and the costs are manageable. But it affects the scope and cost of any works significantly, and it should be priced in from the start.

Something worth knowing

A wall that has been recently repointed with cement can be in worse condition than one that has not been touched in forty years. Cement is impermeable. It traps moisture inside the masonry rather than allowing it to evaporate, which accelerates the decay of both the original lime mortar and, in time, the stone face itself. Fresh grey pointing is not a sign of good maintenance. It can be a sign of the opposite.

The roof

Galicia is one of the wettest regions in Europe. The roof is not a nice-to-have. It is the first line of defence against a climate that will test it every winter without mercy, and a roof that has been failing for even a few years will have caused damage throughout the building that is not always visible on first inspection.

Look at the ridge line from a distance. It should be straight. Any dip or curve suggests the ridge beam or principal rafters have failed or been overloaded. Missing or displaced tiles, even a small area, are worth taking seriously. Water in Galicia has no difficulty finding its way in.

If you can access the loft space, do it. Look for daylight through the roof covering. Look for dark staining on the rafters, which indicates water has been getting in for long enough to stain the timber. Look at the condition of the rafters and purlins themselves. If they are soft, discoloured, or crumbling at the edges, the structural timber has been compromised and you are looking at a complete roof replacement rather than a repair.

Traditional Galician roofs use either lousa (slate) or clay pantiles, depending on the area. Both are good materials, both are locally available, and both are repairable by local contractors at reasonable cost. The tile type is not the issue. The structure underneath it is.

The floors

Almost every traditional rural house in Galicia uses timber floor joists spanning between the external walls. When maintained and kept dry, these structures perform well. When a building has been unoccupied and unheated for a decade or more, they can be in any condition at all, and the only way to know is to look.

Walk the floors carefully and feel for movement. A small amount of flex is normal. A floor that deflects significantly underfoot, or that moves as a whole section rather than locally, tells you that the joists have failed or are failing. The most common failure point is the joist end, where the timber sits in a pocket in the masonry wall and is most exposed to moisture. Check the perimeter of every floor.

Look for small round holes in exposed timber and fine dust on surfaces below. That is woodworm. Active infestations are treatable. Extensive historical damage that has gone untreated for long enough can compromise structural integrity in ways that are not immediately obvious.

A practical note

Never assume a floor is safe to walk on in an unoccupied rural property. Test every area carefully before committing your full weight. Partial floor collapses in buildings that have been empty for years are more common than you might expect, and they happen without warning.

Damp and moisture

Traditional stone buildings in Galicia have no damp proof course. They were designed to breathe, to allow moisture to move through the masonry naturally and evaporate, regulated by lime mortar and lime plaster. This system works well when it is left alone. It breaks down when someone applies impermeable finishes: cement render on the outside, vinyl paint on the inside, closed-cell insulation boards against the walls.

Look for tide marks at low level on internal walls, white crystalline deposits on stone faces (efflorescence, which is mineral salts being carried to the surface by evaporating moisture), and persistent damp patches that show no sign of drying between viewings. These are not automatically disqualifying. But they indicate either ongoing moisture ingress or previous remediation that has made things worse, and both affect the scope of works you will need.

Planning: what you might not be allowed to do

This is the part that catches most buyers out. The assumption is that because the building already exists, you can do whatever you like with it. In Galicia, that assumption is wrong often enough that it needs to be tested before you make an offer, not after.

Solo Rustico and what it means in practice

The majority of rural land in Galicia is classified as Solo Rustico under the Lei do Solo de Galicia. Buildings in this classification can generally be rehabilitated and used for residential purposes. Extensions, new outbuildings, changes of use for tourism, and some structural modifications are subject to strict controls that vary significantly between local authorities.

The specific rules for any given property depend on its sub-classification within Solo Rustico, the policies of the local Concello (municipality), and whether the building or land has any heritage or environmental protection designation. The only way to know for certain is to check the local Plan General de Ordenacion Municipal before committing.

If you are planning to use the property as a tourist rental or rural hotel rather than a primary residence, the planning requirements are different again, and in some Concellos considerably more restrictive. Establish your intended use early and check it against the local rules before the price negotiation begins.

Licencia de obras: the permission you need before you lift a trowel

Any works beyond basic maintenance require a licencia de obras from the local Concello. The type of licence required depends on the scope of what you are doing.

Obra menor covers minor works: internal repairs, replacing windows and doors, repainting, basic maintenance. The process is relatively straightforward and timescales of four to eight weeks are typical in most Concellos.

Obra mayor covers anything more significant: structural repairs, roof replacement, changes to the external envelope, new openings, full rehabilitation. This requires a full technical project signed by a registered arquitecto and supervised by an aparejador. Timescales of three to six months are realistic. In some Concellos, longer.

Works carried out without the appropriate licence cannot be regularised in most cases and can prevent future sale, mortgage, or legal use of the property. The licencia process is slow and sometimes frustrating. It is also not optional.

The most common mistake

Buyers complete the purchase, get the keys, and start work immediately because the building is sitting there and the contractors are available. The licencia application goes in at the same time, on the assumption that it will come through before the works reach anything significant. Sometimes this works. When it does not, a stop notice from the Concello halts everything, and the costs of standing still while the paperwork catches up are not trivial. Apply for the licence before works start. Every time.

Unlicensed works: someone else's problem that becomes yours

A significant proportion of rural properties in Galicia have had works carried out without a licence at some point. An extra room added in the 1980s. A barn converted without permission. A second storey added to a single-storey building. These works may or may not appear in the catastro or the escritura.

When they do not appear, you are potentially purchasing a property with illegal structures that become your legal liability the moment you complete. Always compare the physical building against the catastral description. Any discrepancy in floor area, number of storeys, or outbuildings should be investigated before you exchange.

The legal paperwork that actually matters

Start with the nota simple

The nota simple is a basic extract from the Registro de la Propiedad, the Spanish Land Registry. It tells you who the registered owner is, gives the legal description of the property, and shows any charges, mortgages, or legal encumbrances registered against it. It costs around nine euros and can be ordered online at registradores.org.

Request it as early as possible, ideally before a second viewing. It will not tell you everything you need to know, but it is the essential first check and there is no good reason to wait.

Catastro versus escritura

The catastro is the national cadastral register, used primarily for taxation. The escritura is the title deed, registered at the Registro de la Propiedad. In theory, both should describe the same property in the same terms. In practice, for rural properties in Galicia, they frequently do not agree, particularly for older properties where informal changes have accumulated over generations without being formally recorded.

Discrepancies are common and not always fatal, but they need to be resolved before completion. Resolution sometimes requires a formal boundary survey, a declaracion de obra nueva for unregistered buildings, or an expediente de dominio to regularise title. All of these take time and cost money. Factor them into your purchase timeline and your budget.

Inheritance and co-ownership

Rural property in Galicia passes through families in ways that create legal complexity over time. Successive inheritances without formal partition result in properties that are co-owned between multiple siblings, cousins, and sometimes people who have no idea they have an interest in a building they have never seen.

Before proceeding seriously with any purchase, establish that the seller has clear and unencumbered title to sell. Your notario will verify this at completion, but discovering six weeks before exchange that there are additional co-owners who have not consented to the sale is an expensive and time-consuming problem. A good gestor or abogado can check this early in the process for a modest fee.

What a proper pre-purchase inspection covers

There is no Spanish equivalent of the RICS HomeBuyer Report. What you commission needs to cover more ground than a standard UK survey, because the planning and legal context is part of the risk picture in a way that it simply is not for a suburban semi in Manchester.

A thorough pre-purchase inspection for a rural Galician property should give you a clear picture of the structural condition, the likely cost of the works you intend, any planning constraints that affect what you can do, and any apparent discrepancies between the physical building and its legal description. The output should be a written report in a language you can read and act on.

If your inspector produces a report in Spanish that you cannot fully understand, you are only partially informed. And partial information, in a transaction this complex, is a reasonably reliable route to a nasty surprise.

On costs

Rural renovation in Galicia is meaningfully cheaper than equivalent works in the UK, particularly on labour. A good local builder working in stone is a genuine craftsperson and not expensive by UK standards. The gap narrows on specialist trades and on materials with long lead times. Budget for a 20 percent contingency on any renovation project. Rural renovation has a way of surfacing things that were not visible from the outside, and the ones that surface halfway through are always the inconvenient ones.

The short version

Buying a rural stone house in Galicia is one of the more rewarding things you can do with a reasonable amount of money and a genuine appetite for a project. The buildings are beautiful, the materials are excellent, the landscape is extraordinary, and the local building tradition is one worth engaging with seriously.

The mistakes that catch people out are not mysterious. They are structural conditions that a trained eye would catch on a first visit. Planning constraints that would have taken an afternoon to check. Legal complications that a nota simple and a competent gestor would have flagged before anyone signed anything.

Go in informed, get the right people around you early, and the rest follows.
